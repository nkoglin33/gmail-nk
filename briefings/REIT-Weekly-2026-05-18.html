THIS WEEK'S READ

The week ending May 17, 2026 saw the retail REIT universe pull back across all five segments, with elevated 10-year Treasury yields (~4.26%) and macro uncertainty weighing on rate-sensitive assets; Net Lease bucked the severity at ‑0.6% while Malls lagged at ‑2.5%, and Grocery-Anchored held at ‑1.5%, reinforcing the segment's defensive positioning. Grocery-anchored fundamentals delivered the clearest operating signal this week: Regency's record 96.6% leased occupancy, Brixmor's 6.4% same-property NOI growth, and Kimco's record new lease spreads of 24% confirm that open-air necessity retail is repricing aggressively and signed-but-not-commenced pipelines represent durable earnings visibility into 2027. Private-market cap rates for best-in-class grocery centers continue to compress toward 5.25–5.50%, while public-market implied cap rates remain ~25–32 basis points wide of that floor — a spread that has historically presaged public-market multiple expansion as private transaction volumes accelerate. For Regency specifically, record leased occupancy, a $635 million development pipeline at 9%-plus blended yields, and the issuance of $450 million in 7-year unsecured notes at the company's lowest-ever credit spread collectively suggest Regency is entering the deployment phase of the cycle from a position of structural advantage.

SEGMENT CROSS-SECTION

 

Convenience / Strip — Curbline and PECO Carry the Segment; SITC Exits

Curbline posted the standout Q1 2026 result in the segment with 4.8% same-property NOI growth, a 96.3% leased rate, and straight-line new lease spreads of 55.9% — reflecting the scarcity premium on high-quality convenience parcels with limited supply in dense trade areas. Phillips Edison matched or exceeded expectations with 3.5% same-center NOI growth, 97.1% occupancy, and comparable new spreads of 36.2%, then raised its full-year NOI guidance to 3.0%–4.0%, signaling management confidence. Acadia's strip fund business delivered roughly 6% same-store growth underpinned by street-retail leasing in urban corridors, while SITE Centers continues its court-ordered asset disposition process with only six wholly-owned centers remaining and a stock price down nearly 60% YTD. Segment cap rates have widened modestly as institutional buyers price in SITC-related supply and broader rate uncertainty, but best-in-class convenience assets continue to command sub-6% pricing in primary markets.

Read-through for Regency: The spread between CURB and PECO new lease economics (36%–56%) versus Regency's own 14.2% cash spreads reflects format differences — convenience parcels reprice more aggressively on shorter TI bases — but confirms the underlying demand signal: retailers are paying dramatically above expiry for well-located, high-traffic open-air space, which supports Regency's mark-to-market runway on the 27% of its ABR rolling over the next three years.

Grocery-Anchored — Operating Outperformance Widens; Brixmor Takes the NOI Crown

Brixmor delivered the strongest same-property NOI print in the segment at 6.4% year-over-year — fueled by record blended cash spreads of 27% (new: 42%, renewal: 21%) — and raised full-year SS NOI guidance to 4.75%–5.5%, the highest in the segment. Regency reported 4.4% same-property NOI growth and a record 96.6% leased occupancy, surpassing its prior 2016–2017 peak, with a $42 million signed-but-not-commenced rent pipeline providing high-visibility earnings acceleration. Kimco posted record Q1 leasing with 576 leases totaling 4.4 million square feet at blended cash spreads of 11.3% and new lease rents near $29/sqft, the highest on record, while InvenTrust delivered solid 2.6% SS NOI growth driven by embedded escalations in its Sun Belt-weighted portfolio. Implied cap rates for the segment sit around 5.68%, about 18–43 basis points above private-market pricing for best-in-class grocery-anchored centers, a gap that is narrowing as institutional capital reenters the market.

Read-through for Regency: BRX's 370bp leased-to-billed spread and KIM's $42 million in signed-not-commenced ABR parallel Regency's own $42 million pipeline at the same stage of the cycle, validating that the entire grocery-anchored subsector is sitting on a significant near-term earnings ramp as 2024–2025 leasing converts to rent commencement; Regency's 37% of that pipeline ($15.5M) commencing within 2026 provides a clear compounding path to Q3 and Q4 NOI acceleration.

Power / Diversified — FRT Leads on FFO Growth; KRG Executing Buybacks

Federal Realty delivered the sharpest FFO per-share growth in the Power segment at +10.6% year-over-year to $1.88 per share, with comparable POI growth of 5.1% on a cash basis and total revenue rising to $341 million; the company is hosting an Investor Day on May 21, likely to outline capital recycling and redevelopment pipeline targets. Kite Realty reported Q1 FFO of $0.52 per share, same-property NOI growth of 3.6%, and 94.7% leased occupancy, while executing $152 million of share repurchases at sub-NAV pricing, with Baird subsequently raising its price target to $28. Urban Edge was the YTD price leader in the segment at +14.0%, supported by strong NOI growth and a Northeast-heavy portfolio that benefits from dense trade area barriers to entry. All three issuers trade at a modest discount to NAV (0.95x–0.98x), reflecting the segment's mixed format exposure and somewhat higher leverage profiles versus pure-play grocery-anchored peers.

Read-through for Regency: FRT's 5.1% cash comparable POI growth sets a favorable peer benchmark supporting Regency's 3.5%–3.75% same-property NOI guidance as conservative; the gap between segment leaders and Regency's guidance range suggests Regency may have room to outperform its stated guidance range, particularly as $15.5 million of signed rent commences in 2026.

Mall / Lifestyle — SPG Raises Guidance; Macerich Dilutes with Equity Offering

Simon Property Group surpassed Q1 2026 expectations with real estate FFO of $3.17 per share (+7.5%), raised full-year FFO guidance to $13.10–$13.25 per share, and increased its dividend by 7.1% to $2.25 per share — all signals that Eli Simon's first quarter at the helm prioritized capital returns and confidence messaging. Tanger delivered steady outlet performance with +3.2% same-store NOI and 8.6% YTD total returns, benefiting from value-oriented consumer preferences amid tariff-related spending uncertainty. Macerich reported 1.2% same-store NOI growth — the weakest in the segment — and priced a $403 million equity offering at $21.00 per share (near the current stock price), signaling balance sheet recapitalization over growth; the offering is dilutive near-term but improves the company's 8.5x net-debt-to-EBITDA profile. Mall cap rates average 7.03%, reflecting the segment's leverage, shorter WALT, and continued structural vulnerability to department store lease-up risk.

Read-through for Regency: The gap between Mall implied cap rates (7.03%) and Grocery-Anchored (5.68%) has widened to 135 basis points, reinforcing Regency's pricing advantage when competing for capital allocation from institutional REIT investors; SPG's strong results confirm consumer spending resilience in quality retail formats, which supports the grocery-anchored thesis of essential, high-frequency foot traffic.

Net Lease — Best Relative Performer; Acquisitions Repricing at 7.3%–7.5%

The Net Lease segment was the clear relative winner this week at ‑0.6% versus ‑1.5% to ‑2.5% for open-air peers, as duration-sensitive investors rotated toward longer-WALT, single-tenant assets in a rising 10-year Treasury environment. NNN REIT held nearly flat on the week (‑0.1%), investing $145 million in Q1 at a 7.5% initial cash cap rate — a level well above where REIT-implied yields price private grocery-anchored assets — while maintaining 98.6% occupancy and raising AFFO guidance to $3.53–$3.59. Realty Income deployed $1.67 billion in Q1 acquisitions and expanded its loans-receivable book to $2.67 billion, signaling continued diversification beyond core retail. Agree Realty (ADC) continues to trade at a premium to NAV (1.05x) on the strength of its investment-grade tenant concentration and clean balance sheet at 4.5x net debt to EBITDA. The segment's acquisition cap rate of 7.3%–7.5% (NNN, FrontView guidance) highlights the divergence between single-tenant and multi-tenant private market pricing.

Read-through for Regency: The 180–200 basis point spread between Net Lease acquisition cap rates (7.3%–7.5%) and Regency's development yields (9%+) confirms that Regency's ground-up and redevelopment pipeline is generating a substantial risk-adjusted premium relative to market alternatives, underscoring why the $600M+ in-process development pipeline is the company's highest-return capital deployment vehicle right now.

THE REAL ESTATE LENS

 

SIGNAL 1

Grocery Anchor Supply Surge — A Structural Tailwind

▲ BULLISH

The collapse of the Kroger-Albertsons merger has triggered a leasing arms race: grocery chains are planning ~21 million sqft of new-store openings in 2026, and Kroger alone is accelerating new-store builds by 30% while Publix continues expanding its northern footprint. This competitive re-leasing dynamic is directly beneficial to grocery-anchored REITs — particularly Regency — as operators compete for the best-located, highest-traffic centers, providing both renewal leverage and new anchor opportunities in Regency's development pipeline markets like Dallas, Denver, and Atlanta.

  

SIGNAL 2

Private Cap Rate Compression vs. Public Discount

▲ BULLISH

Best-in-class grocery-anchored centers are now trading in the private market at 5.25%–5.50% cap rates, while public-market implied cap rates for the same format sit ~28bps higher at 5.68%. Institutional investors and REITs are re-entering the retail allocation — evidenced by the ShopOne/Pantheon grocery-anchored portfolio transaction — and this public-private gap has historically closed via public multiple expansion rather than private cap rate widening. As financing conditions improve (markets pricing 1–2 Fed cuts by year-end), the gap should narrow further, providing a valuation catalyst for grocery-anchored REITs.

  

SIGNAL 3

Debt Cost Improvement Accelerating REIT Deployment

▲ BULLISH

Average REIT unsecured debt coupon fell to 4.7% in Q1 2026 from 5.4% in Q1 2025 — a 70bps improvement in a single year — and Regency's $450M notes at 4.5% represent the tightest credit spread in company history, unlocking accretive capital for development and acquisition at returns (9%+ development, 5.50% implied cap) that remain comfortably above the cost of capital. With the Fed funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% and the 10-year at ~4.26%, cap rate compression of 25–50bps is widely expected by year-end 2026, which would add 5%–10% to stabilized asset values.

REGENCY PORTFOLIO READ-THROUGH

 

SUN BELT & MIDWEST MARKETS

This week's data reinforces the bifurcation within the Sun Belt: while CBRE and ICSC note modest rent-growth deceleration in oversupplied Sun Belt submarkets, Regency's portfolio skews toward the highest-barrier, infill nodes within Atlanta, Dallas, Denver, and Tampa — markets where grocery-anchored center supply remains effectively zero and incumbent landlords enjoy pricing power well above market average. InvenTrust's 2.6% SS NOI growth (a Sun Belt-pure portfolio) confirms that embedded escalations dominate in markets with stable occupancy, while Regency's superior 4.4% print suggests its infill positioning commands a measurable premium. In the Midwest, Chicago's grocery-leasing boom — with Whole Foods, Aldi, and Trader Joe's all signing new locations post-merger collapse — directly benefits Regency's Chicago-area centers and creates inbound leasing demand for its development pipeline in the Indianapolis and Minneapolis corridors.

GROCERY ANCHOR HEALTH

The grocery anchor picture is broadly positive for Regency's tenant roster. Kroger — Regency's largest anchor — is accelerating new-store builds by 30% in 2026, including Marketplace-format openings in Dallas (Anna, Little Elm, Fort Worth) that are squarely in Regency's target development markets, while 60 weaker-format closures are expected to be confined to non-infill locations that do not directly threaten Regency's centers. Publix is actively expanding northward into Virginia and Kentucky, creating anchor demand for Regency's development pipeline assets in mid-Atlantic and Midwest markets. Albertsons, following the failed merger, is competing directly against Kroger for prime anchor spots, which structurally improves Regency's negotiating leverage on renewal terms. Whole Foods and Trader Joe's continued aggressive Chicago-area leasing signals sustained premium grocery demand in Regency's Midwest operating base. No Regency anchor tenants appear on any current credit watch list; the pharmacy exposure risk remains isolated to REITs with heavy CVS/Walgreens concentrations, which is not a meaningful Regency exposure.

LEASING & SMALL SHOP TRENDS

The peer leasing data this week provides Regency with a highly favorable read-through on its small shop NOI recovery trajectory. Brixmor's small shop occupancy hit 92.1% (+130bps YoY) with a $67M signed-not-commenced pipeline at $24/sqft — 25% above in-place ABR — confirming that small shop demand is compounding well above asking-rent inflation across the grocery-anchored format. Kimco's record new lease rents of $29/sqft and Curbline's 55.9% new lease spreads both confirm that the structural undersupply of quality open-air small shop space is allowing landlords to capture years of deferred rent escalation in a single lease event. For Regency, the 230bp leased-to-commenced spread ($42M of ABR awaiting commencement) represents the most visible near-term earnings driver: 37% ($15.5M) commences within 2026 and 87% ($36.5M) by end of 2027, implying a base-case NOI compounding story that is largely already signed. Small shop national retailers — including fitness, health & wellness, and service providers — continue to absorb open-air space at above-trend velocity, with 2026 store openings expected to reach a three-year high.

ACQUISITION & DEPLOYMENT

The week's private-market data and peer earnings prints collectively validate Regency's current capital deployment posture. Best-in-class grocery-anchored center acquisitions are pricing at 5.25%–5.50% in the private market (ShopOne/Pantheon transaction, ICSC/CBRE data), which is approximately 40–75 basis points inside Regency's own 5.75%–6.00% development yields for redevelopment completions — meaning Regency can create grocery-anchored value at a meaningful discount to where it would acquire it. The $635 million in-process development pipeline at 9% blended yield — with over $1 billion in project-start potential over the next three years — represents Regency's highest-conviction capital allocation: development returns are roughly 350bps above current private-market acquisition cap rates, and each 25bps of future cap rate compression adds ~$150M of portfolio value. With leverage near the low end of the 5.0–5.5x target range and unsecured debt costs at a record trough, Regency has both the balance sheet capacity and the cost-of-capital advantage to selectively accelerate new project starts through the back half of 2026 as construction pipeline pricing stabilizes.
