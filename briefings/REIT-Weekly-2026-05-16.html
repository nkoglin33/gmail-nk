THIS WEEK'S READ

Grocery-anchored and convenience-strip REITs led the public retail universe for the week ending May 16, with broad-based strength following a strong Q1 earnings season in which 59% of reporting REITs raised full-year FFO guidance — well above the historical 40–45% rate. Operating fundamentals remain the story: Curbline's 55.9% new-lease spread and PECO's 36.2% comparable new-lease spread underscore the pricing power in necessity-based open-air formats, while Kimco's record 576-lease quarter at $29/sf new rents signals durable grocery-anchored demand. The public-to-private cap rate gap narrows modestly as best-in-class grocery-anchored assets transact in the 5.25–5.50% range versus implied public-market caps in the high-5s, compressing the arbitrage window but validating portfolio quality. For Regency, the 230bp leased-to-commenced spread representing $42M of signed-but-not-commenced ABR provides exceptional near-term NOI visibility, reinforcing management's above-6% total NOI growth target for 2026.

SEGMENT CROSS-SECTION

 

Convenience / Strip — Curbline and PECO Carry the Segment on Record Spread Data

Curbline Properties (CURB) reported Q1 same-property NOI growth of 4.8% with straight-line new lease spreads of 55.9% and renewal spreads of 14.7%, while deploying $142M into acquisitions — underscoring the thesis that necessity-based convenience retail is absorbing institutional capital at pace. Phillips Edison (PECO) matched with comparable new-lease spreads of 36.2% (record inline spread of 37.9%) and 3.5% same-center NOI growth, lifting its 2026 Core FFO outlook and reaching 97.1% portfolio occupancy. Both names trade within 3% of NAV, with implied caps in the 6.0–6.1% range against private-market grocery-convenience transactions printing 5.25–5.50% in primary markets. SITE Centers trades at a 12% discount to NAV at a 6.80% implied cap, reflecting ongoing execution risk as the company repositions its portfolio post-spin.

Read-through for Regency: The 55.9% new-lease spread at Curbline and 36.2% at PECO validate the pricing power in sub-anchor strip formats that Regency's own shop-space leasing program is capturing — 16.8% blended spreads at REG suggest meaningful embedded rent-to-market that can be harvested through 2026–2027 lease expirations. The private-market cap rate for best-in-class convenience retail at 5.25–5.50% compresses the acquisition arbitrage for Regency but is simultaneously a confirmation signal on portfolio valuations.

Grocery Anchored — Kimco Posts Record Quarter; Regency Signals $600M+ Pipeline at 9% Yield

Kimco Realty (KIM) delivered a record Q1 with 576 leases totaling 4.4M square feet at blended cash spreads of 11.3% — new leases alone climbed 23.8% year-over-year to a record $29/sf, with net income rising to $157.4M ($0.23/diluted share) versus $125.1M in Q1 2025. Regency Centers (REG) reported base rent growth of 3.5%, same-property NOI guidance of 3.25–3.75% for 2026, and total NOI growth expected to exceed 6% supported by its $635M in-process development and redevelopment pipeline at a blended 9% yield. A 230bp spread between leased (96.6%) and commenced (94.3%) occupancy represents $42M of near-term incremental ABR, providing exceptional forward NOI visibility. Brixmor (BRX) posted 4.0% same-property NOI growth with record lease spreads of 17.8%, while InvenTrust (IVT) continued to compound its Sun Belt grocery-anchored exposure with 2.9% SS NOI growth and conservative 4.8x net debt/EBITDA leverage.

Read-through for Regency: Kimco's record new-lease rents and Brixmor's 17.8% renewal spreads are the strongest sector-wide validation of Regency's own leasing strategy — peer data supports REG's double-digit cash rent spread execution as sustainable rather than cyclically peak. The $42M of signed-not-commenced ABR gives management high confidence in above-6% NOI growth regardless of macro noise.

Power / Diversified — Federal Realty Leads on FFO Growth; Segment Carries More Leverage Risk

Federal Realty (FRT) was the segment standout, reporting a 10.6% year-over-year increase in FFO per share in Q1 2026, earning an analyst upgrade to Buy and scheduling a May 21 Investor Day to outline its long-term capital allocation priorities. FRT's implied cap of 5.95% reflects the premium attributed to its mixed-use infill assets in high-barrier coastal markets, and its 3.8% SS NOI growth led the power/diversified cohort. Kite Realty (KRG) continues to integrate its large portfolio of 27.3M square feet in suburban Sun Belt and Midwest markets with steady 3.0% SS NOI growth and 13.8% lease spreads. Urban Edge (UE) carries elevated leverage at 6.1x net debt/EBITDA, limiting capital deployment flexibility, though its urban-adjacent assets in the Northeast generate durable demand. The segment-wide implied cap of 6.32% versus 5.88% for grocery-anchored peers reflects the market's preference for anchor-quality covenant over power-center diversity.

Read-through for Regency: FRT's 10.6% FFO growth and premium cap rate reinforces that grocery-anchored mixed-use formats command the highest institutional valuations — a tailwind for Regency's $635M redevelopment pipeline that increasingly tilts toward mixed-use and experiential retail additions alongside grocery anchors.

Mall / Lifestyle — Simon Raises Guidance 7%; Pennsylvania REIT Remains the Outlier

Simon Property Group (SPG) was the week's best performer with a 2.4% 5-day return, buoyed by its Q1 report showing real estate FFO up 7.5% year-over-year to $1.2B, full-year FFO guidance raised to $13.10–$13.25, and a 7.1% dividend increase to $2.25/share — demonstrating that trophy-mall cash flows remain highly resilient to e-commerce pressure at the luxury and premium outlet tiers. Tanger (SKT) maintains a healthy 5.2x leverage profile and 9.8% lease spreads in outlet retail, while Macerich (MAC) trades at an 18% discount to NAV with high leverage at 8.2x net debt/EBITDA, creating an ongoing overhang even as SS NOI improved to 3.1%. Pennsylvania REIT (PEI) remains the sector outlier: 11.2x leverage, 8.50% implied cap, −2.1% YTD return, and structurally impaired balance sheet — the lone STRAINED name in the universe this week.

Read-through for Regency: SPG's premium outlet and luxury anchoring is not a direct comp, but its FFO growth trajectory signals consumer spending health at the higher end — a read-through to the aspirational small-shop tenants (apparel, food, wellness) that increasingly populate Regency's shop-space roster and drive marginal NOI upside.

Net Lease — Solid Earnings Season Muted by Rate Sensitivity; Spreads Thin vs. Open-Air

The net lease segment trailed all other retail REIT segments this week with a +0.9% average 5-day return, reflecting continued sensitivity to the 10-year Treasury at 4.27% and modest lease spread dynamics (net lease structures typically limit annual rent growth to 1.5–2.0%). Realty Income (O) raised its 2026 AFFO guidance to $4.41–$4.44 after a Q1 beat of $1.13/share AFFO on $1.55B revenue, though YTD return of just 3.2% reflects the rate headwind and share-count pressure from its diversification push into Europe and data centers. NNN REIT raised AFFO guidance midpoint by $0.01 to $3.56. Agree Realty (ADC) and Essential Properties (EPRT) outperformed within net lease on cleaner balance sheets (4.2x and 4.8x leverage respectively) and 5.4% projected AFFO growth at ADC. The segment-wide implied cap of 5.74% reflects tight single-tenant underwriting but limited NOI upside versus open-air grocery-anchored formats generating 10–18% lease spreads.

Read-through for Regency: Net lease's 5.74% implied cap versus Regency's 5.70% implies the market is pricing open-air grocery-anchored with equivalent or superior risk-adjusted quality to investment-grade single-tenant — a favorable valuation backdrop that supports REG as a core holding and validates premium pricing in any disposition or JV structures Regency might pursue.

THE REAL ESTATE LENS

 

SIGNAL 1

Cap Rate Compression Resuming in Grocery-Anchored

▲ BULLISH

Best-in-class grocery-anchored assets in primary markets are now transacting at 5.25–5.50% cap rates — a 25–50bp tightening from mid-2025 levels — as institutional buyers (including private equity at $2.3B A&B deal) compete for limited quality supply. The 70bp gap between public implied caps (~5.70–5.90%) and private transaction pricing signals ongoing NAV upside for grocery-anchored REITs as the spread normalizes. For Regency, every 25bp of cap rate compression adds approximately $1.50–2.00/share to estimated NAV.

  

SIGNAL 2

Grocery Anchor Expansion Outpacing Closures

▲ BULLISH

Kroger is accelerating new-store builds by 30% in 2026 with $200M in Central Division investment, new Marketplace formats in North Texas, and expanded footprints in Kentucky and Indiana. Publix added three more Kentucky leases. Nationally, grocery chains plan nearly 21M square feet of new stores in 2026 — the strongest expansion pipeline in a decade, fueled by the Kroger-Albertsons merger collapse redirecting growth capital to organic expansion. Albertsons' restructuring creates selective re-tenanting opportunities for well-located centers but is manageable for REITs with diversified anchor rosters. The net impulse for grocery-anchored REITs is strongly positive: anchor health supports renewal leverage and new-deal economics.

  

SIGNAL 3

Debt Cost Improvement Supports Development Returns

— NEUTRAL

REIT unsecured debt costs averaged 4.7% in Q1 2026 versus 5.4% in Q1 2025 — a meaningful 70bp improvement that directly benefits development return hurdles and acquisition financing economics. The 10-year Treasury remains sticky at ~4.27% with 1–2 Fed cuts priced for H2 2026, providing a gradual tailwind rather than a step-change relief. For Regency's $635M development pipeline underwritten at 9% blended yields, current debt costs create a 420–430bp development spread — healthy by historical standards. The structural upward pressure on long-term rates from elevated Treasury supply remains the primary residual risk to cap rate expansion assumptions.

REGENCY PORTFOLIO READ-THROUGH

 

SUN BELT & MIDWEST MARKETS

This week's data reinforces the divergent narrative between Sun Belt supply and Midwest stability that is directly relevant to Regency's core market exposure. While headline analysis flags oversupply in certain Sun Belt multifamily submarkets, grocery-anchored retail in Atlanta, Dallas, and Denver continues to benefit from population-driven household formation and minimal competitive new supply — net absorption in necessity-based retail remains positive across these markets in 2026. Kite Realty's steady 3.0% SS NOI growth across its 27.3M-sf predominantly Sun Belt and Midwest portfolio provides a direct operational read-through: tenants are renewing and expanding, and there is no sign of demand softness in grocery-anchored suburban formats. Chicago and Minneapolis, core Midwest markets for Regency, are consistent performers aligned with broader ICSC data showing the Midwest maintaining stable rent growth patterns while Sun Belt markets absorb incremental supply. Regency's leasing team should continue to prioritize market-rate renewal negotiations in its high-barrier coastal and Midwest assets where replacement supply is constrained.

GROCERY ANCHOR HEALTH

Kroger is the dominant theme this week: the company is accelerating new-store builds by 30% in 2026 with $200M committed to its Central Division, including new Marketplace format openings in North Texas, Indiana, and West Virginia — a direct positive signal for Regency centers anchored by Kroger banners. Post the failed $24.6B Kroger-Albertsons merger, Kroger is redirecting capital from M&A toward organic large-format growth, and this benefits Regency in two ways: existing Kroger anchor leases gain renewal leverage as the grocer expands footprint, and new Marketplace development sites could include Regency-affiliated developments in growth markets. Publix continues its Midwest and Southeast push with new Kentucky lease signings. The offsetting risk is Albertsons: the company announced store closures and layoffs in April 2026 as part of a restructuring — Regency centers with Albertsons exposure (primarily Safeway and Vons banners on the West Coast) should be monitored for potential re-tenanting opportunities that could ultimately generate positive NOI events through mark-to-market re-leasing at current spreads of 16–18%.

LEASING & SMALL SHOP TRENDS

Peer lease spread data this week is uniformly bullish for Regency's own leasing pipeline. PECO's 36.2% new-lease spread and 21.2% renewal spread, Brixmor's 17.8% record spreads, and Kimco's 11.3% blended spread collectively confirm that embedded rent-to-market across the grocery-anchored universe — including Regency — is substantial and being systematically harvested through lease expirations. Regency's own 16.8% blended spread and 94.1% shop-space leasing occupancy (96.6% leased overall) shows the company is tracking at or above peer medians. The 230bp leased-to-commenced gap at REG ($42M incremental ABR) is a forward-year NOI reserve that peers with tighter leased/commenced spreads do not possess, providing a structural NOI growth runway independent of new leasing activity. Small-shop categories driving demand across the peer set — health and wellness, food service, beauty, and specialty grocery — align directly with Regency's tenant roster priorities and support continued shop-space occupancy gains through year-end 2026.

ACQUISITION & DEPLOYMENT

The private-market transaction data this week presents a nuanced acquisition backdrop for Regency. Best-in-class grocery-anchored assets in primary markets are pricing at 5.25–5.50% cap rates — a level that compresses the development spread relative to Regency's 9% underwritten yields on its existing pipeline, but does not eliminate it. The DLC/DRA $429M acquisition at implied mid-6% caps on value-add power/community center product suggests that secondary-quality retail remains in a different pricing tier, creating selectivity opportunity for Regency to acquire well-located grocery-anchored assets that trade wide of the market due to short lease term, credit issues, or operational underperformance. Curbline's $142M Q1 deployment into convenience retail at competitive economics confirms that well-capitalized buyers are active — Regency's 5.2x leverage and investment-grade balance sheet position it to compete for acquisitions without dilutive equity at current valuations. The $1B three-year development start target ($333M per year) at 9% blended yields remains the highest-return capital allocation for Regency and should be prioritized over acquisitions at current private-market pricing.
