THIS WEEK'S READ

Grocery-anchored and convenience-strip REITs again led the public retail universe this week as a dense Q1 2026 earnings calendar confirmed what operators have signaled for months: occupancy is at or near cycle peaks, lease spreads remain decisively positive, and the development pipeline is re-accelerating on disciplined yields. Regency Centers crossed a new all-time leased high of 96.6%, Phillips Edison reported comparable new spreads of 36% while lifting its full-year outlook, and Kimco posted record signed-but-not-open ABR of $77 million — collectively validating the thesis that supply-constrained, necessity-anchored formats hold structural pricing power. The public-to-private cap rate gap for convenience and grocery-anchored product has narrowed materially from the 112-basis-point spread observed in mid-2025, with best-in-class private transactions now clearing in the 5.25–5.50% range and public implied caps converging near that band, removing the acquisition-premium headwind that weighed on deployment economics last year. For Regency specifically, the convergence is constructive: the company's $600 million-plus in-process development pipeline at blended returns above 9% and freshly restated capacity for $1 billion in starts over 2026–2028 position it to capture the best risk-adjusted spread between current cost of capital and stabilized cap rates that the sector has seen since the 2017–2019 cycle peak.

SEGMENT CROSS-SECTION

 

Convenience / Strip — Acadia and PECO Carry the Segment on Record Spreads

Acadia Realty posted the week's single best 5-day return at +4.0%, driven by Q1 2026 results showing same-store NOI growth of approximately 5.9% and economic occupancy of 94% — with the urban portfolio up 570 basis points year over year. Phillips Edison similarly outperformed after reporting Q1 same-center NOI of +3.5%, comparable new lease spreads of 36.2% (inline new leases at a record 37.9%), and total portfolio occupancy of 97.1%, prompting management to lift its full-year guidance. Curbline continued to build momentum as a pure-play convenience-center platform, with 10 acquisitions completed for $111.4 million year-to-date through Q1 and a lengthening pipeline of off-market and lightly marketed opportunities. SITE Centers, now effectively a wind-down vehicle following the completion of the Curbline spin-off, lagged the group and is excluded from fundamental comparisons. Private-market pricing for convenience and necessity-anchored strip product remains firm, with best-in-class assets transacting at 5.25–5.50% cap rates, while public implied caps have converged to near 5.45% on average — a narrow spread that suggests limited further multiple expansion from public-private arbitrage alone.

Read-through for Regency: PECO's record new-lease rents of $29+ per square foot and 36% comparable spreads confirm that tenant demand for well-located necessity retail is structurally intact, validating Regency's own double-digit spread execution; and the Curbline private-transaction pipeline illustrates that off-market sourcing remains viable in the 5.25–5.50% cap range — useful calibration for Regency's own convenience-format acquisition underwriting.

Grocery-Anchored — Regency Hits New Leased Peak; Kimco Sets SNO Record

The grocery-anchored segment posted a 5-day return of +2.1% as Q1 2026 results across the group confirmed that occupancy is no longer a recovery story — it is at or beyond prior peaks. Regency crossed 96.6% leased occupancy, surpassing its 2016–2017 high, with 1.5 million square feet of comparable leasing at double-digit cash rent spreads and a development pipeline exceeding $600 million at blended returns above 9%. Kimco reported record signed-but-not-open ABR of $77 million — a $10 million increase from year-end — and raised its full-year same-site NOI growth outlook to 2.8–3.5%, noting that Q1's +1.7% was the expected trough. Brixmor extended its momentum, delivering a Q1 beat with recent trailing lease spreads of 17.8% and same-property NOI growth of approximately 4%; YTD total return of +16.4% leads the sub-segment. InvenTrust, the smallest of the four, is benefiting from Sun Belt demographic tailwinds with limited comparable data available for Q1 but trades at a modest 0.98x P/NAV, representing a potential relative-value entry point. Best-in-class private grocery-anchored cap rates sit at 5.25–5.50%, with public implied caps converging to approximately 5.56% — a gap that has essentially closed versus the 112bp spread observed in mid-2025.

Regency's own all-time leased-occupancy record and $600M-plus development pipeline at 9%+ blended returns position it as the highest-quality read on the segment's operating upswing; the closed public-private cap rate gap also means the acquisition market is no longer a headwind, and the projected $1 billion in development starts over 2026–2028 provides a visible path to above-peer NOI growth over the next three years.

Power / Diversified — Federal Realty Lifts the Segment on a 10.6% FFO Beat

Federal Realty delivered the segment's earnings highlight, reporting Q1 2026 FFO of $1.88 per share (+10.6% year-over-year) with comparable property operating income growth of 4.7% and raising its full-year guidance to Core FFO of $7.46–$7.55 — the widest upward revision in the segment this quarter. Kite Realty traded modestly higher (+1.0%) and is tracking in-line with its long-term leasing pipeline, while Urban Edge (+0.5%) is generating solid embedded NOI from a recent urban-edge acquisition program but carries a wider implied cap rate of 6.25% reflecting mixed asset quality. The average implied cap rate for the segment sits at approximately 6.08%, roughly 50–60 basis points wide of the grocery-anchored cohort — a premium that reflects diversified market exposure, variable anchor quality, and in some cases higher leverage. The RPT Realty portfolio was fully integrated into Kimco in 2023 and is now contributing to that platform's suburban grocery-anchored footprint rather than reported as a standalone entity. Institutional investment appetite for power and diversified product is improving but remains more selective than for pure grocery-anchored, with a preference for high-density suburban locations and limited big-box vacancy risk.

FRT's 4.7% POI growth confirms that mixed-use and street-retail formats with high-quality anchors can generate above-average NOI lift, consistent with Regency's own mixed-use and urban-infill developments; the 50bp+ cap rate premium for the power segment also validates Regency's strategic focus on grocery-anchored as the tightest-cap-rate, lowest-risk format in open-air retail.

Mall / Lifestyle — Simon Steady; Macerich Leverage Remains the Overhang

The mall segment posted a modest +1.0% five-day return as investors awaited Simon Property Group's Q1 2026 earnings, scheduled for May 11, with SPG trading at approximately $202.96 and tracking a 34% gain over the trailing twelve months on the strength of its premium-mall leasing and international exposure. Tanger (+1.5%) has benefited from the value-retail tailwind, with off-price and discount tenants continuing to execute store-opening programs that fill its outlet footprint; same-store NOI growth of approximately 3.0% and lease spreads of 14% make it one of the steadier operators in the group. Macerich continues to carry the highest Net Debt/EBITDA in the covered universe at 7.5x and trades at a 15% discount to estimated NAV, reflecting ongoing concern about its B and B+ mall exposure and the pace of anchor retenanting; the company's 7.00% implied cap rate stands out as the widest in the combined retail REIT universe outside of legacy restructured formats. PREIT went private in 2023 following its second Chapter 11 restructuring and is excluded from public comparisons. Saks Global's late-stage financial distress — including a missed $100 million interest payment and a reported search for a $1 billion emergency loan — is the most acute near-term watch item for mall operators with luxury-apparel exposure.

Mall and grocery-anchored retail are diverging at the tenant-credit level: the Saks Global distress that threatens to disrupt mall anchor slotage has zero direct exposure for Regency, whose anchor roster is dominated by investment-grade grocers; conversely, the strong performance of value, off-price, and service tenants at Tanger and SPG outdoor formats validates the tenant mix Regency is assembling in its small-shop and pad space.

Net Lease — NNN Raises Guidance; Realty Income Lags on Q1 Timing

The net lease segment was the week's weakest cohort at +0.3% as Realty Income (-1.0%) traded cautiously ahead of its May 6 Q1 earnings report, with analysts modeling $1.10 EPS against a backdrop of slowing acquisition volume and a 9.5% one-month stock decline into the print. NNN REIT delivered a solid beat-and-raise, reporting Q1 EPS of $0.50 in-line with estimates on a revenue beat, raising full-year AFFO guidance to $3.53–$3.59, and confirming occupancy of 98.6% with ABR up 6.9% year-over-year — the strongest operational quarter in the net lease group. Agree Realty trades at 15.83x forward P/FFO and 4.5x Net Debt/EBITDA, the lowest leverage among net lease issuers and a reflection of its disciplined, investment-grade-tenant-focused growth strategy. Essential Properties continues to differentiate with its service-and-experience tenant focus (carwashes, convenience stores, early education), posting stable metrics and a balanced $4.0x leverage profile. Spirit Realty was fully merged into Realty Income in January 2024 and its assets are now reflected in O's consolidated portfolio. Average net lease implied cap of approximately 6.13% represents a meaningful premium to grocery-anchored, reflecting the sector's lower operating leverage and passive structure rather than lower asset quality.

The net lease segment's average 6.13% implied cap rate is 57 basis points wide of the grocery-anchored cohort, a spread that creates a favorable relative-value argument for grocery-anchored REITs like Regency; as interest rates stabilize and cap rates potentially compress, the active-management premium embedded in Regency's development and redevelopment platform becomes an incrementally larger differentiator versus passive net lease vehicles.

CAPITAL MARKETS

  • REIT Debt Markets — Q1 2026 sector-wide debt issuance totaled $6.3B with an average coupon of 4.7%, down 70 basis points from the 5.4% average in Q1 2025, reflecting tighter spreads and a more accommodating credit environment; overall REIT capital offerings of $10.0B in Q1, down from $12.2B in the prior-year period as equity issuance lags behind valuation recovery.
  • Rate Environment — The Federal Reserve held the fed funds rate at 3.50–3.75% at its January 2026 meeting; the 10-year Treasury stands near 4.27%, producing a roughly 314bp spread to average retail REIT implied cap rates and maintaining favorable refinancing economics for issuers with staggered debt maturities; commercial mortgage rates opening the week near 5.33%.
  • CRE Refinancing Pressure — Approximately $875B in commercial and multifamily mortgage balances mature in 2026 (17% of the total market), sustaining elevated refinancing activity across the sector; REITs with predominantly fixed-rate, long-dated debt (industry average: ~90% fixed, 6.4-year WAM) are relatively insulated, but private-market owners face more acute roll risk that continues to motivate portfolio sales.

TRANSACTIONS

  • ShopOne / Grocery-Anchored Portfolio Sale — Town Lane, in partnership with a leading international institutional investor, acquired ShopOne Centers and a 27-asset grocery-anchored portfolio from Pantheon; no pricing was disclosed but the deal validates continued institutional appetite for the format at a point in the cycle where public-private spread has largely closed.
  • Curbline (CURB) Acquisitions — Curbline reported it had acquired 10 convenience shopping centers for $111.4M year-to-date through Q1, consistent with its stated strategy of deploying capital at a pace well above the $200M annual target set at the time of the SITE Centers spin-off; the platform has maintained low leverage (Net Debt/EBITDA ~2.1x) while building an off-market transaction pipeline.
  • Regency Redevelopment Starts — Regency started $73M of new redevelopment projects in Q1 2026, including Crystal Brook Corner on Long Island — a full transformation into a Whole Foods-anchored neighborhood center — and completed $42M of ground-up and redevelopment deliveries; the active pipeline now exceeds $600M with blended returns above 9%, representing the most aggressive development posture since the pre-pandemic cycle.

TENANT CREDIT

  • Saks Global — WATCH — Saks Global is in a 30-day grace period after missing a $100M interest payment as of January 8, 2026; key vendors have halted shipments and the company is reportedly seeking a $1 billion emergency loan; exposure is concentrated in Class A malls and luxury-adjacent lifestyle centers — minimal direct exposure for grocery-anchored REITs but a signal that high-end fashion anchor demand has not returned to pre-Covid levels across the full quality spectrum.
  • Torrid — DOWNGRADE — S&P downgraded Torrid after the apparel chain disclosed closure of 74 additional locations and reiterated plans for further reductions; the capital structure was described as unsustainable; Torrid's tenant base (plus-size apparel, strip and power center adjacent) overlaps with lower-tier convenience strip and community center portfolios — minimal Regency exposure.
  • Value Retailers Expanding — Dollar General, Aldi, and Tractor Supply top planned store-opening lists for 2026; grocery chains nationally are planning nearly 21M sq ft of new stores (CBRE) as post-Kroger-Albertsons merger collapse frees capital for organic expansion by Publix, H-E-B, Whole Foods, and Trader Joe's — a direct positive for grocery-anchored REIT rent rolls and lease renewal leverage.
  • Store Closures Trend Down — U.S. retailers are projected to close approximately 7,900 stores in 2026, a 4.5% decline year-over-year and the lowest closure count in three years; net absorption is expected to remain positive, with value, off-price, service, and grocery retailers absorbing vacated space and in many cases bidding against each other for well-located storefronts.

THE REAL ESTATE LENS

 

SIGNAL 1

Public-Private Cap Rate Convergence

▲ BULLISH

The 112-basis-point spread between public REIT implied caps and private grocery-anchored transaction rates that defined 2025 has effectively closed, with private best-in-class grocery centers now clearing at 5.25–5.50% and public implied caps converging near 5.35–5.56%. For Regency, this removes the NAV-discount acquisition headwind and means the company can deploy into assets — at development yields above 9% — where the spread to market cap rates is the widest it has been in years, compressing payback periods and improving blended return profiles on new development starts.

  

SIGNAL 2

Grocery Anchor Expansion Accelerates Post-Merger

▲ BULLISH

The collapse of the Kroger-Albertsons merger in December 2024 has released a wave of grocery capital into new-store leasing, with CBRE estimating nearly 21 million square feet of new grocery leases planned for 2026. Chains including Whole Foods, Trader Joe's, Publix, H-E-B, and Aldi are actively competing for well-located anchor boxes in high-household-income suburban markets — precisely the trade area profile that defines Regency's core Sun Belt and Midwest portfolio. This demand surge strengthens renewal leverage for Regency's approximately 80% grocery-anchored asset base and supports above-market rent bumps on upcoming expirations.

  

SIGNAL 3

Debt Costs Easing; Maturity Wall Drives Private-to-Public Flow

— NEUTRAL

The Fed held at 3.50–3.75% while the 10-year Treasury sits near 4.27%, producing a roughly 314-basis-point spread to average retail REIT cap rates — supportive of current valuations but not yet catalytic for multiple expansion. REIT unsecured debt coupons averaged 4.7% in Q1 2026 versus 5.4% a year prior, improving refinancing economics for investment-grade issuers like Regency. The $875 billion private CRE maturity wall in 2026 creates continued distress-sale opportunities from over-levered private owners, though execution will favor buyers with pre-positioned acquisition platforms and long-standing broker relationships.

REGENCY PORTFOLIO READ-THROUGH

 

SUN BELT & MIDWEST MARKETS

This week's grocery expansion data is particularly constructive for Regency's core Sun Belt and Midwest footprint. CBRE estimates 21 million square feet of new grocery leasing nationally in 2026, with the bulk targeting high-household-income suburban submarkets — the exact profile of Regency's Atlanta, Dallas, Denver, Chicago, and Minneapolis clusters. The post-Kroger-Albertsons merger reactivation of organic growth strategies by Publix and H-E-B is especially notable: both chains have historically avoided each other's geographies, but Publix's northward march into the Midwest and H-E-B's Dallas-Fort Worth saturation push will intersect with Regency's Texas and Florida portfolios over the next 12–18 months, creating anchor renewal and new-lease opportunities at premium rents. CBRE's 2026 retail outlook confirms Sun Belt oversupply in apartment deliveries but explicitly carves out grocery-anchored retail as supply-constrained and outperforming — a distinction that supports Regency's above-average occupancy trajectory in its highest-growth markets.

GROCERY ANCHOR HEALTH

Grocery anchor health is the cleanest read-through from this week's data. The collapse of the Kroger-Albertsons merger freed both chains — and their competitors — to pursue aggressive independent store programs, and the result is the most active grocery leasing pipeline in over a decade. Whole Foods demonstrated commitment to the format with Regency's own Crystal Brook Corner redevelopment on Long Island, where the chain is anchoring a full neighborhood center transformation with a new 45,000+ square foot store. Publix continues to rank as the nation's highest-rated supermarket chain by customer satisfaction and is actively expanding into non-traditional geographies including the Midwest — a meaningful tailwind for Regency's Chicago and Minneapolis portfolios where Publix has limited existing presence. Albertsons, now operating independently post-merger collapse, resumed capital investment in store remodels and reported stable renewal intent across its lease book; there are no material non-renewal signals in the current data. Whole Foods and Trader Joe's store-count expansions in high-income suburban corridors directly reinforce the demand dynamic underpinning Regency's development pipeline returns.

LEASING & SMALL SHOP TRENDS

Peer lease spread data from Q1 2026 confirms that the small-shop NOI recovery is not only intact but accelerating into what may be its strongest year yet. Phillips Edison's record comparable inline new leases at 37.9% spreads and Kimco's blended 11.3% spreads — against a record SNO pipeline of $77 million — suggest that the lease-to-commencement gap will continue to burn off meaningfully through 2026 and into 2027, adding incremental same-store NOI with low incremental capex. Regency's own double-digit cash rent spreads and 96.6% leased occupancy (vs. 94.3% commenced) imply a $150+ million embedded NOI pipeline from signed-but-not-yet-commenced leases — growth that requires no additional leasing to be executed, simply time. The broader data also confirms that service tenants (healthcare, fitness, personal services, restaurants) continue to be the most competitive bidders for inline space, supporting rent growth even as goods-retail foot traffic stabilizes. Store closures projected at a three-year low in 2026 reduce the risk of involuntary vacancy disrupting Regency's occupancy runway.

ACQUISITION & DEPLOYMENT

The closed public-private cap rate gap is the most important deployment signal of the week for Regency. With private best-in-class grocery-anchored assets trading at 5.25–5.50% and Regency's development yields above 9%, the spread-to-market on new development has widened to a cycle-best level of approximately 350–375 basis points — the most compelling new-start economics since 2019. The $875 billion commercial mortgage maturity wall creates a parallel stream of acquisition opportunities from over-levered private owners who lack refinancing flexibility, and Regency's investment-grade balance sheet (Net Debt/EBITDA ~5.4x, 90%+ fixed rate) positions it as a natural buyer in a market where distressed sellers are increasingly motivated. The Curbline platform's ability to source 10 convenience center acquisitions at implied caps of 5.25–5.50% in Q1 alone demonstrates that off-market deal flow exists at scale — and that Regency's grocery-anchored version of that sourcing advantage, cultivated over decades of operator relationships in the Southeast, Sun Belt, and Midwest, should continue to surface attractive deal flow throughout 2026 and beyond. Management's stated $1 billion in development starts over 2026–2028 implies an average annual run rate of ~$333 million — three times the 2020–2022 average — which at 9%+ blended yields will generate meaningful above-market NOI growth that should compound REG's already-elevated valuation premium relative to peers.
